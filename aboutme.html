Team

  • Locality and simplicity - systems must be loosely coupled, features should be delivered fast and independently.
  • Focus, flow, and joy - work on small batches and get continual feedbacks rather than waiting for other people to get things done on our behalf.
  • Improvement of daily work - if there is a choice between a new feature and developer productivity, the latter should always be chosen.
  • Psychological safety - there must be no fear for speaking up about what I know and what I feel.
  • Customer focus - the value and impact of our work on our customer must always be considerd.

Organization

  • Horizon 1 - manage to value, focus on consistency, rules, compliance. Predictable and well-known operation model.
  • Horizon 2 - new customers, adjacent market, different business model.
  • Horizon 3 - manage to growth, focus on constant experiementation. Must break rules and processes governing Horizon 1.
  • Left unchecked, Horizon 1 leaders can consume all resources of the company leaving nothing for Horizon 3.

Leadership

  • Influence others to follow the lead, getting them to participate, and helping them improve.
  • Laissez faire - hands-off management with limited communication but expects high accountability.
  • Consultant - decision making involves the team members and weighting the options.
  • There is no place for dictatorship and micro-management.
  • Light shall shine to people making most contribution to the team.
  • Take account of skills and interest when doing the work.
  • Build on strength rather than weakness.
  • Focus on those who have ability and desire. (Passion and ownership cannot be taught)
  • Speak to the team before making changes that will impact them.
  • Set clear and measurable expectations, document everything, and provide on-going feedbacks.
  • Be transparent on bad news.

Lessons from experience

  • What business told me was rarely what they actually wanted. Requirements were never written in a way that delivered context. I realized I needed interpret the requirements correctly by contiune asking questions.
  • Mixing up with agile and waterfall was a mistake because we had fixed set of deliverables by certain date while allow new items to come into the sprint. And we end up infinite amount of work. If items come in, then new items need to come out.
  • Manual testing and deployment hit the limit quickly as the application became more complex. We insisted "due deligence" of human effort but we quickly realized that everyone makes mistakes. Automation is not only recommened, it is necessary.
  • When we were estimating the work effort, giving a range of time only made business assume the shortest time. I learned to consider the worst case scenario to always make promise that I can deliver, and also apply that principle when doing VSM.
